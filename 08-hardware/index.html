8 · FFT hardware explorer

From the maths to the silicon. The same radix-2 FFT, seen as hardware blocks, and how the identical maths maps to three different FPGA layouts. Click into each level. (Part 1, hardware)

Why this matters: a practical FFT core has to be power-efficient. Power and area live in the physical blocks (multipliers, memories, data movement) and in the architecture choice (how much you build versus how much you reuse). This explorer makes those concrete.

Level AThe butterfly as hardware
One radix-2 butterfly in silicon: complex multiplier, adder, subtractor. Drill into the multiplier to see a complex multiply = 4 real multiplies + 2 adds (and the 3-multiply trick).
Level BThe supporting blocks
What surrounds the butterfly: a twiddle ROM (constants, not computed live), a RAM for samples and intermediate results, and control / address-generation logic.
Level CThree FPGA architectures
The area-versus-throughput knob: iterative (one butterfly, reused), pipelined (one per stage, streaming), parallel (all at once). Resource counts for a chosen N make the tradeoff concrete.

The thread: all three architectures compute the same maths. They differ only in how many physical butterflies you build and how you schedule the data through them. That is the heart of a configurable, power-efficient core.
